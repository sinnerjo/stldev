Game model

A clear way of playing that matches the philosophy. The model favors purposeful possession, strong transitions, and compact defending with steady pressure on the ball.

Formations by format

Game format Preferred shape Notes
7v7 2 3 1 Two central defenders, three across the middle, one central forward
9v9 3-2-3 Back three, two central midfielders, two wide midfielders, three forwards with wide threats
11v11 4 3 3 Back four, three in midfield, three forwards with wide threats

In possession

The ball is used on purpose. Possession is not just keeping it, it is keeping it to create chances.

Principles in possession
  • Play forward when it makes sense and has support
  • Use the first pass after winning the ball to attack quickly when possible
  • Keep the ball with intent rather than passing without a plan
  • Support the ball with smart angles and distances
Transitions into attack
When the team wins the ball, the first thought is to move forward and attack with support. Players look to break lines, combine, and reach the goal with purpose, while teammates give options around the ball.

Out of possession

The team stays compact and always applies pressure on the ball.

Defensive shape
The team defends in a compact block that protects central spaces. Lines move together and the gap between defenders and midfielders stays small.
Pressure on the ball
There is always at least one player pressing the ball carrier. Teammates provide cover and balance, ready to step in if the first defender is beaten or if the ball is passed.

Transitions

Attack transition
On winning the ball, the priority is to move forward with support. Players recognize space behind or between opponents and look to use it quickly, while keeping enough help near the ball to keep the attack alive if the first option closes.
Defense transition
When possession is lost, the team gets compact and applies pressure on the new ball carrier immediately. This slows the opponent, gives time to recover shape, and can create chances to win the ball back in good areas.

Link to training

The game model does not sit on a whiteboard. It guides how sessions are built, which scenarios are used in practice, and the language coaches use with players. Activities mirror the decisions and pictures players will see on match day.
