Training model

How sessions are built to match the philosophy and game model. The structure is repeatable, flexible, and focused on decision making, creativity, and technical quality for U15 and younger.

Session blueprint

Most sessions follow a clear rhythm so players know what to expect while still feeling challenged and creative.

Play
Arrival activity
Players start working as soon as they step onto the field using small sided games (2v2, 3v3, ...) We start (and end) with the player's favorite part of every training session... play. It sets a positive and engaging tone for the entire training.
Practice
Non-Game Form
Non-game form activities isolate a specific idea or technical action without the full pressure of a game. These can include footskills, first touch, shooting, passing where the technique is applied outside of an activity or game. They give players clear repetitions that support the decisions they’ll make later in the session.
Practice
Main activity
The main activity stretches the picture into a larger space, more game like situation that requires not only technical expertise but decision making as well. Here, the idea is tied directly to the game model, such as finishing from a through pass or attacking out of a transition.
Play
End as you started
Sessions end with a game that looks like match play. This give the players the opportunity to try out their lesson in the day's activity in a realistic game setting.

Weekly rhythm

Over a week, themes are balanced so players see repeated ideas in different pictures.

Day one foundation
Focus on core habits such as first touch, support angles, and simple decision making in possession games. Ideal for rondos, small sided possession, and basic pressing cues.
Day two game model link
Focus on a clear part of the game model. Examples include finishing from through balls, attacking quickly after winning the ball, or defending as a compact unit.
Optional third day themed or fun focus
Used for lighter, creative sessions such as fun themed nights, recovery days, or positive culture activities that keep players engaged and fresh.

Key training principles

Decision first activities
Activities are chosen because they force decisions. Players work out when to pass, dribble, or shoot instead of repeating pre set patterns. The coach helps them see options instead of giving every answer.
Creativity stays welcome
Players are encouraged to try new solutions, even if they fail at first. Mistakes are treated as normal parts of training. A poor choice only becomes a problem if it repeats with no learning.
Technical work inside the game
Most technical training is built into game like situations instead of isolated drills. Players improve first touch, passing, receiving, and finishing while reading pressure and teammates.
Attacking emphasis with defensive habits
Many activities begin from attacks, counter attacks, or finishing situations. At the same time, defending principles such as pressure on the ball and compact shape are always present.

Examples of training themes

Themes repeat across the year so players see the same ideas with different variations.

Finishing from through passes
Arrival activity with simple passing patterns, a rondo to work on angles, and a main game where midfielders play forwards in behind. The session links decision making on the pass with timing of the run and finish.
Transition to attack
Games begin with recovering the ball and moving forward quickly with support. Coaching points include first pass after winning the ball, movement of the nearest teammates, and choices in the final third.
Compact defending with pressure on the ball
Small sided games where teams earn points for winning the ball in certain zones. The focus stays on one player pressing the ball while teammates stay tight and ready to cover.

Age appropriate adjustments

Younger ages U5 to U10
More time in arrival play and small sided games, shorter explanations, and simple language. The same training structure is used, but activities feel like fun games with hidden learning inside.
Older ages U11 to U15
More detail in coaching points, clearer links to the game model, and stronger expectations for focus. Players are invited to take ownership of warmups and reflections over time.

Connection to the lab

The training model is how the philosophy and game model show up on the field. It keeps enjoyment high, demands real decisions, and helps players grow toward the next level while staying in love with the game.
